## Overview

Ukaleq is worshipped as the god of death, the dead, and the afterlife. Though his domain carries fear and finality, he is widely regarded as a benevolent presence. To most souls, Ukaleq is not an executioner, but a guide, greeting the newly departed with patience and clarity as they leave the world behind.

Ukaleq teaches that death is not a punishment, but a transition. Life gains meaning because it ends, and endings allow renewal to exist at all. He does not hurry the living toward their deaths, nor does he mourn them excessively. He simply ensures that when death comes, it is honored and guided.

Those who attempt to defy the natural boundary between life and death draw his ire. Necromancy that enslaves souls, undeath that denies final rest, and attempts to hoard existence beyond its rightful span are seen by Ukaleq as violations, not of morality, but of reality itself.

---

### Depictions in Art and Myth

Ukaleq is depicted as a towering skeletal figure clad in regal attire of obsidian, precious metals, and dark gemstones. His form is imposing but dignified, evoking authority rather than terror. His skull bears intricate gold carvings, and a halo of marigolds rests above it, symbolizing both mourning and remembrance.

His empty eye sockets are said to peer directly into the essence of a soul, perceiving not judgment in the moral sense, but readiness. Those who live in harmony with mortality often feel serenity in his presence. Those who cling desperately to life feel unease, sensing truths they would rather avoid.

Artists frequently portray Ukaleq standing at thresholds, gates, or crossroads, reinforcing his role as a guide rather than a ruler. He is never shown chasing the living, only waiting.

## Goals

Ukaleq’s purpose is to preserve the sanctity of death. He ensures that souls pass onward without interference, exploitation, or delay. His role is not to punish the dead, but to prevent the living from corrupting the boundary between life and what comes after.

He believes death is a gift, one that grants urgency, meaning, and perspective to mortal lives. Without death, existence stagnates, and identity erodes. Ukaleq works to uphold this truth quietly, without spectacle or coercion.

Ukaleq opposes undeath not out of hatred, but out of mercy. To bind a soul to a decaying vessel or deny it passage is, in his view, a cruelty greater than death itself. Those who practice such acts knowingly place themselves in direct opposition to him.

He does not seek worship, dominion, or expansion. Death comes to all regardless. Ukaleq’s task is simply to be there when it does.

## Relationships

Ukaleq stands apart from most divine rivalries, not through isolation, but through inevitability. All paths, divine or mortal, eventually pass through his domain, and this grants him a perspective few gods can share. He does not compete for influence. He waits.

Ukaleq’s relationship with Cyra is one of deep, unresolved sorrow. Of all the gods, she grieved his death most fiercely. As goddess of creation and family, she felt his loss not as a cosmic shift, but as a personal wound. Though Cyra accepts Ukaleq’s role as arbiter of the dead, she never truly reconciles with the necessity of death. Ukaleq understands this without resentment. He does not ask forgiveness for what he represents, nor does he demand acceptance. Their bond is defined by love that endures despite disagreement.

He maintains a quiet and respectful accord with Calypso. Where Calypso governs the unfolding of life and fate, Ukaleq governs its conclusion and passage beyond. The two rarely conflict, as death is not a contradiction of time, but its fulfillment. Mortals often pray to Calypso for long seasons and to Ukaleq for peace at the end, rarely realizing how closely the two forces are intertwined.

Ukaleq holds deep disdain for undeath and any perversion of the soul’s passage. This places him in firm opposition to Xalzotec. Where Xalzotec binds souls through blood and shadow, Ukaleq seeks release and clarity. Their conflict is not theatrical, but absolute. To Ukaleq, undeath is not merely evil, but obscene, a denial of meaning itself.

Despite Asmodeus’s role in manipulating the events that led to his death, Ukaleq does not hold personal hatred for Asmodeus. Death, in Ukaleq’s view, is neither punishment nor injustice. This dispassionate stance frustrates Asmodeus, who prefers enemies driven by guilt or rage. Ukaleq instead judges Asmodeus not for his schemes, but for how they twist souls into tools rather than allowing them their final rest.

Ukaleq shares an understanding, if not affection, with Pestilina. Disease, poison, and famine often serve as thresholds to his domain. Ukaleq does not condemn Pestilina’s work outright, but neither does he celebrate it. Where she tests endurance through suffering, Ukaleq receives those who fail those tests without judgment.

He respects Nanuk for his acceptance of death as an outcome rather than a moral failure. A hunt ends. A life ends. Nanuk does not flinch from this truth, and Ukaleq values that honesty. Their domains intersect often, but without rivalry.

Ukaleq’s relationship with Cyrus is measured and restrained. Justice governs how lives are lived. Ukaleq governs what follows. He does not interfere in judgment, but he ensures that no sentence extends beyond death itself. Even gods, in his view, do not own souls once they have passed.

He holds little patience for Rodikar, though he does not oppose him directly. Rodikar glorifies death in battle. Ukaleq strips it of glory. To Ukaleq, death is not a victory, nor a failure. It simply is.

Ukaleq views Thalia with gentle approval. Joy, love, and story give meaning to mortality. Lives worth mourning are lives well lived. Thalia’s influence ensures that death is not empty, and for that, Ukaleq is quietly grateful.

Among the pantheon, Ukaleq is neither feared nor loved in the way other gods are. He is accepted. He is the one who opens the door when all others close. And though he is remembered as the god whose death began the Sundering, Ukaleq himself does not dwell on that moment. He has eternity to walk others through theirs.

## Myth and History

Ukaleq’s origins are a mystery even to scholars of the divine. Records of him before [[the Sundering]] are sparse or nonexistent, yet the gods are said to have cared for him deeply. His death marked the beginning of the Sundering and shattered the divine order.

Asmodeus, whispering into the fractured mind of his brother Komaku, drove him into paranoia and fear. In his madness, Komaku killed Ukaleq. The death of a god sent shockwaves through reality, unraveling the balance of life and causing the gods to retreat from the world in grief and shock.

Not long after his death, Ukaleq reached out from beyond, speaking to the gods from the other side. He described death not as loss, but as clarity. As release. As a blessing that allowed existence to matter.

Moved by this revelation, the gods returned with a new understanding. Death would no longer be an accident or catastrophe, but a universal truth. Ukaleq was restored, not to life, but to purpose, set as the arbiter of souls and guide to the afterlife.

From that moment on, death became part of the natural order of Faysel, and Ukaleq its calm and unwavering steward.
